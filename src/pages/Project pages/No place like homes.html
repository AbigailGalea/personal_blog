No place like homes.

Growing up, I have always lived in Malta, an archipelago of three tiny islands in the middle of the Mediterranean. In the summer of 2020 I moved to Norrkoping in Sweden to pursue my Master’s degree at Linkoping University. I left Malta and two flights later I was in Stockholm. I spent the next year and a half living and exploring Sweden. I found these two countries culturally quite different. I found myself in new situations and thinking in new ways. I made a lot of new friends along the way and missed the ones back in Malta. After this experience, I find myself at a crossroads- I am unsure of where to call home. Through this exhibition I wanted to capture the essence of my memories in both places. When faced with this dilemma, where do you call home?

This project was made using Unity Engine (2021.2.7f1) with some assets being made and imported from Blender, MetaShape (by Agisoft) as well as online sources. For some parts of the production, online tutorials for Unity were also followed to achieve the desired effects. The following subsections will explain the process of making and putting together everything. Firstly a brief explanation of how the first person controller was made will be given. The set up of space and portal will be explained. Then an explanation will be given for how the height maps of the countries were made. The process of making the picture frames in Blender will be explained. I will also describe briefly how the spin animation was added to the boat model and in the following subsection an explanation of how the mirrors on the wall were made and rendered will be given. An explanation of how photogrammetry was used to create the 3D model of the pastizzi will be given. A short description of the user interface will be given.

Give it a try and read about the process of making it below!

First person controller (FPC)

To make the first person controller which the user could use to explore the exhibit, I followed Brackey’s tutorial on YouTube for building an FPC. To summarise the process; one first has to create a player object with a character controller component and capsule which acts as the player’s body. Then one adds a camera which will act as the viewport (i.e. player’s eyes) and aligns it to where the eyes of the player would be.

A script was added for controlling the movement of the camera using the mouse. The main idea of this script is that as the mouse is moved left and right, the entire player body is spun around. When the mouse is moved up or down the vertical angle of the camera is shifted. Combining these two functions together allows the player to look around them (360° on the x-axis and 180° on the y-axis).

Furthermore, another script was used to allow the player to move around using the arrow keys. Essentially this script took key presses as controls to transform the location of the player within the world, taking into consideration the current location and looking direction.

Portal set-up

When exploring the space, one is able to transition between Malta’s exhibit and Sweden’s exhibit by passing through a portal. To set this up, another tutorial by Brackeys was followed.

The set-up starts off with two areas being created. Portals are then added to both rooms and flat planes are placed inside them facing opposite directions. Similar to the process of making mirrors (explained later in the mirrors section), the planes used camera images to constantly render and show what the other world looked like based on where the player was positioned and looking.

Using scripts and an additional collider plane in every portal, the player was then allowed to move from one world to another. When the player body collides with the plane inside the portal from the correct direction, their body is moved to the location of the other plane with a few adjustments to make the transition feel natural.

Height maps

Using black and white 2D height maps available online for Malta and Sweden, Blender was used to build 3D height maps. The process involved importing the 2D images into Blender and then using their pixel brightness as an indicator of height. The darkest (completely black) pixels were displaced most along the y-axis. This process gave a 3D map of heights in different places.

Picture frames

Making the picture frames in Blender was a relatively simple process. Two cubes were resized and shaped and then one was subtracted from the other using the boolean operator to give the frame shape. A different material was given to the face which would hold the image and the model was exported to Unity.

Boat spinning animation

To create the boat spinning animation I followed a short tutorial by Windows. The basic idea of the script which spins the boat, is that at every rendered frame, the rotation of the boat is changed along the chosen axis by a given amount (based on speed chosen).

Mirrors

When looking at the boat model in the Malta side of the museum, visitors are able to see themselves as a capsule in a mirror, as they move around, so does their character in the mirror surface. This was done using a camera facing outwards from the mirror surface projecting onto a render texture which was applied to the mirror’s material. This way, the camera was able to take in a video input of the scene and concurrently display it on the mirror’s surface.

Photogrammetry asset

The 3d model of the pastizzi was made using MetaShape by Agisoft, following the instructions given in the course by Masaki Hayashi. 19 pictures of the pastizzi were taken from various angles using a Huawei mobile phone camera. Then using the workflow menu the photos were aligned and a dense point cloud was built based on that. Based on the dense cloud, a mesh was built as well as a texture based on the pictures. The model was then briefly edited in Blender, to remove the flooring, and imported into the Unity project as a model with a texture.

Controls UI

To allow users to be able to see what controls they need to move around in the environment and control their character, a small interface was added. The user could press a button at the bottom left corner (“Controls”) and they would get instructions on how to operate their character. By pressing the same button again they could then hide the panel again and continue viewing the museum. This was done by attaching a short script to a UI button object, which activated and deactivated the controls help panel depending on the current status.

Description of final product

The final product of this work was a 3D virtual museum project in Unity. Visitors to this virtual museum could walk around the space and explore the different pictures and 3D models. Upon entering the museum, the visitor sees a wall of pictures of Malta, on a traditional limestone brick wall, and a floor covered in traditional Maltese tiles. To their left they see a portal arch that leads to Sweden’s exhibit, ahead of them, a 3D model of a traditional Maltese fishing boat called a Kajjik (Fig.3.2).

The pictures on the walls seek to capture life on the Maltese islands whereas the height map (Fig. 3.3) indicates what the topology of the landscape looks like. The scanned model of traditional food, a pastizz, represents culture and day to day life (Fig.3.4).

When the visitor is done looking around Malta’s exhibit they can then walk through the arch to Sweden. Here visitors can again look at pictures of Sweden as well as the height map of the land. As for a Swedish tradition, I chose to present a Dalahäst asset (fig. 3.5) which I downloaded from an online website. This model reminds me of life in Sweden because I got one as a Christmas gift from one of my friends in Sweden and I always keep it in my room.

Evaluation

To review how the museum was received, the project was made available online at the following link:
https://play.unity.com/mg/other/webgl-builds-131003
The link was then sent to family and friends and as of the time of writing this report, the game had 32 views, 30 plays, 6 comments and 3 (positive) reactions. The feedback collected through the comments section was also positive, indicating that the online visitors had enjoyed the experience. Through private messages, some commented that they were unsure about how to control the character, for this reason I added the controls button in the UI of the final project. I also moved the labels for the 3D models from the side to the top since some people informed me that they were not easy to read in their initial position on the side.

This project showcased the work undertaken over the span of a few months as well as pictures gathered over the past years. In this personal collection, the museum visitors could hopefully get a better feeling for what life is like in Malta and in Sweden, the colours that colour both places and the distinct styles that make both countries so beautiful and unique.

After working on this project I have found that I really enjoyed using the Unity engine and I feel that I have a better understanding of how virtual spaces can be used to showcase works interactively. I have also learnt a lot of new skills within the engine.

To allow others to experience and enjoy this from their own computer, I made the project available using Unity’s Share WebGL package. I found the comment functionality on the website to also be useful for getting feedback from the online museum visitors.
